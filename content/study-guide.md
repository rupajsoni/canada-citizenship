## [ ] MODULE 1 — Rights & Responsibilities
> The 1982 Charter is the backbone of everything. Know its categories cold — they love asking "which type of right is X?"

### Key Facts
- **Canadian Charter of Rights and Freedoms** is part of the **Constitution Act, 1982**
- **6 categories of rights:**
  1. **Fundamental freedoms** — expression, religion, peaceful assembly, press, association
  2. **Democratic rights** — vote and run for office (18+)
  3. **Mobility rights** — live and work anywhere in Canada, enter/leave freely
  4. **Legal rights** — fair trial, no unreasonable search, right to a lawyer
  5. **Equality rights** — no discrimination based on race, sex, age, disability, religion, etc.
  6. **Language rights** — English and French are official languages
- Rights come **with responsibilities** — this is a core test theme
- **Official Languages Act (1969):** English and French are Canada's two official languages
- **New Brunswick** is the only officially bilingual province
- **Quebec** is the only province with a majority French-speaking population

### Responsibilities of Citizens
- Obey Canadian laws
- Pay taxes
- Vote in elections
- Serve on a jury when called
- Respect the rights and freedoms of others
- Help others in the community
- Defend Canada if needed

### QnA — Module 1
**Q: What document protects the rights and freedoms of Canadians?**
A: The Canadian Charter of Rights and Freedoms (part of the Constitution Act, 1982)

**Q: Name two official languages of Canada.**
A: English and French

**Q: Which is the only officially bilingual province?**
A: New Brunswick

**Q: What are two fundamental freedoms protected by the Charter?**
A: Freedom of expression (speech) and freedom of religion (any two of: expression, religion, peaceful assembly, press, association)

**Q: At what age can Canadians vote?**
A: 18 years old

**Q: Name three responsibilities of Canadian citizenship.**
A: Any three of: obey laws, pay taxes, vote in elections, serve on a jury, respect others' rights, help the community, defend Canada

**Q: What type of right is the right to live and work anywhere in Canada?**
A: Mobility right

**Q: What type of right is the right to a fair trial?**
A: Legal right

**Q: When did the Charter of Rights and Freedoms come into effect?**
A: 1982 (Constitution Act, 1982)

**Q: What is the equality right in Canada?**
A: Every individual is equal before and under the law, without discrimination based on race, national/ethnic origin, colour, religion, sex, age, or mental/physical disability

## [ ] MODULE 2 — Who We Are (Indigenous & Diversity)
> Canada's diversity story starts with Indigenous peoples — the test heavily features First Nations, Métis, and Inuit. Know the three groups and their distinctions.

### Key Facts
- **Three groups of Aboriginal (Indigenous) peoples:**
  1. **First Nations** — largest group, hundreds of nations, live across Canada
  2. **Métis** — mixed European and First Nations heritage, mainly Prairie provinces
  3. **Inuit** — live in the Arctic (Nunavut, NWT, northern Quebec, Labrador); "Inuit" means "the people"
- Aboriginal peoples have rights under **Section 35 of the Constitution Act, 1982**
- **Residential schools** — forced assimilation policy, a dark chapter in Canadian history
- **Famous Métis leader:** Louis Riel (led Red River Resistance 1869–70)
- Canada is officially **multicultural** — Multiculturalism Act (1988)
- Population ~38 million (know this is approximate; don't memorize exact number)
- Two-thirds of Canadians live in the **Quebec City–Windsor corridor**
- Canada accepts ~250,000–300,000 new immigrants per year (largest per-capita in the world)
- **Commonwealth:** Canada is a member — 54 countries once part of British Empire
- **La Francophonie:** international organization of French-speaking nations — Canada is a member

### QnA — Module 2
**Q: What are the three main groups of Aboriginal peoples in Canada?**
A: First Nations, Métis, and Inuit

**Q: What does the word "Inuit" mean?**
A: "The people"

**Q: Where do the majority of Métis people live?**
A: Prairie provinces (Manitoba, Saskatchewan, Alberta)

**Q: Who was a famous Métis leader?**
A: Louis Riel

**Q: Where do most Canadians live?**
A: In cities; the majority live in the Quebec City–Windsor corridor

**Q: What does Canada's Multiculturalism Act recognize?**
A: That Canadians of all backgrounds are equal and that cultural diversity is a fundamental value

**Q: What are Aboriginal rights based on?**
A: Section 35 of the Constitution Act, 1982

**Q: What is the Commonwealth?**
A: An association of 54 countries, most of which were once part of the British Empire

## [ ] MODULE 3 — History I: Beginnings to Confederation (1497–1867)
> This is dense but testable. The key thread: French settle → British win → they figure out how to coexist → Confederation. Know the dates and names.

### Key Facts — Exploration
- **1497** — **John Cabot** (sailing for England) became first European since Vikings to reach mainland North America; first to draw a map of Canada's east coast
- **1534** — **Jacques Cartier** sailed up the St. Lawrence, claimed Canada for France
- **1608** — **Samuel de Champlain** founded **Quebec City** (first permanent European settlement)
- **New France** — French colony; fur trade was the economic engine; alliances with First Nations

### Key Facts — British Take Over
- **1759 — Battle of the Plains of Abraham** — British General Wolfe defeated French General Montcalm outside Quebec City; defining moment
- **1763 — Treaty of Paris** — France gave Canada to Britain; French Canadians became British subjects
- **1774 — Quebec Act** — Britain granted French Canadians the right to keep their language, religion (Catholic), and civil law → foundation of French Canada's survival
- **1776** — American Revolution → ~40,000–50,000 **United Empire Loyalists** fled to Canada; they believed in a constitutional monarchy and are considered founders of English Canada
- **1791 — Constitutional Act** — divided Canada into **Upper Canada** (English, Ontario) and **Lower Canada** (French, Quebec); each got elected assemblies
- **1793** — Lt. Col. **John Graves Simcoe**, first Lt. Governor of Upper Canada, made Upper Canada the first province to abolish slavery
- **1812** — British Parliament abolished slavery throughout the Empire
- **1812–1814 — War of 1812** — USA invaded Canada, were repelled; helped shape Canadian identity separate from American; General **Isaac Brock** was a hero
- **1837–38** — Rebellions in Upper and Lower Canada demanding more democracy
- **1840 — Act of Union** — merged Upper and Lower Canada into the Province of Canada
- **1848** — **Responsible government** achieved — first in Nova Scotia; cabinet must have majority support in elected assembly; this is the principle that ministers are accountable to the people

### Key Facts — Confederation
- **July 1, 1867 — Confederation** — Canada became a self-governing Dominion
- **Four founding provinces:** Ontario, Quebec, Nova Scotia, New Brunswick
- **First Prime Minister:** Sir **John A. Macdonald** (Conservative)
- Confederation motto: **"Peace, Order, and Good Government"** (POGG)
- Vision phrase: **"A Mari Usque Ad Mare"** = "From Sea to Sea" (Canada's motto)
- **Fathers of Confederation:** Macdonald, Cartier, Tilley, Tupper, McGee, Brown (know at least 3)

### QnA — Module 3
**Q: Who was the first European to map Canada's east coast?**
A: John Cabot (1497)

**Q: Who founded Quebec City and when?**
A: Samuel de Champlain, 1608

**Q: What happened at the Battle of the Plains of Abraham (1759)?**
A: British forces defeated the French; it ended French military power in North America

**Q: What was the Treaty of Paris (1763)?**
A: France gave Canada to Britain

**Q: What did the Quebec Act of 1774 grant?**
A: French Canadians the right to keep their language, religion (Catholic), and civil law

**Q: Who were the United Empire Loyalists?**
A: People who remained loyal to the British Crown and fled to Canada after the American Revolution (~40,000–50,000 people)

**Q: When did Canada become a self-governing Dominion?**
A: July 1, 1867 (Confederation)

**Q: Name the four founding provinces of Canada.**
A: Ontario, Quebec, Nova Scotia, and New Brunswick

**Q: Who was Canada's first Prime Minister?**
A: Sir John A. Macdonald

**Q: What does "responsible government" mean?**
A: That the Cabinet (ministers) must have majority support from the elected representatives in the House of Commons in order to govern

**Q: Which province was the first to achieve responsible government?**
A: Nova Scotia (1848)

**Q: What is Canada's national motto?**
A: "A Mari Usque Ad Mare" — "From Sea to Sea"

## [ ] MODULE 4 — History II: Building a Nation (1867–1914)
> This era is about westward expansion, the railway, and tensions between English and French Canada. Louis Riel keeps showing up — know him.

### Key Facts
- **1869–70 — Red River Resistance** — Louis Riel led Métis in Manitoba against Canadian government encroachment; led to **Manitoba joining Confederation (1870)**
- **1871** — British Columbia joined Confederation (promised a railway)
- **1873** — Prince Edward Island joined Confederation
- **Canadian Pacific Railway (CPR) — completed 1885** — connected Canada coast to coast; made western settlement possible; Chinese workers contributed enormously (head tax imposed on them after, a dark chapter)
- **Northwest Resistance (1885)** — Louis Riel led another uprising in Saskatchewan; was captured, tried for treason, and **hanged** — hugely divisive; seen as martyr by French Canadians, rebel by English
- **1896–1911 — Sir Wilfrid Laurier** — first French-Canadian Prime Minister; era of great immigration to the West ("the 20th century belongs to Canada")
- **1898** — Klondike Gold Rush in Yukon

### QnA — Module 4
**Q: What was the Red River Resistance (1869–70)?**
A: Louis Riel led the Métis people in Manitoba to resist Canadian government takeover; resulted in Manitoba joining Confederation

**Q: What made it possible for immigrants to settle in Western Canada?**
A: The completion of the Canadian Pacific Railway (CPR) in 1885

**Q: What happened to Louis Riel?**
A: He was tried for treason and hanged in 1885 after the Northwest Resistance

**Q: Who was Canada's first French-Canadian Prime Minister?**
A: Sir Wilfrid Laurier (1896–1911)

**Q: When did BC join Confederation?**
A: 1871 (in exchange for the promise of a railway)

**Q: When was the Klondike Gold Rush?**
A: 1896–1898, in the Yukon

## [ ] MODULE 5 — History III: Wars & Modern Era (1914–2000s)
> Two world wars built Canada's identity and earned its independence from Britain. Know the battles, the dates, and what they mean culturally.

### Key Facts — WWI (1914–1918)
- Canada entered WWI in 1914 as part of the British Empire
- **Vimy Ridge — April 9, 1917** — all four divisions of the Canadian Corps fought together for the first time and captured the ridge; considered the birth of Canadian national identity; a defining moment
- **Passchendaele (1917)** — brutal battle, massive Canadian casualties
- **Women's right to vote (federal): 1918** — Manitoba, Saskatchewan, Alberta granted it in 1916 first
- Over 60,000 Canadians killed in WWI
- **1919 — Winnipeg General Strike** — workers demanded better wages and conditions; one of the largest strikes in Canadian history

### Key Facts — Between Wars
- **1931 — Statute of Westminster** — Canada gained full independence from Britain in foreign policy
- The Depression hit Canada hard in the 1930s

### Key Facts — WWII (1939–1945)
- Canada declared war independently (showed it was a sovereign nation)
- **Battle of the Atlantic** — Canadian navy protected supply ships crossing the ocean
- **Dieppe Raid (1942)** — disastrous but taught Allies lessons for D-Day
- **D-Day — June 6, 1944** — Canadians stormed Juno Beach in Normandy, France
- Over 45,000 Canadians killed in WWII
- Japanese Canadians were interned — another dark chapter

### Key Facts — Post-War & Modern
- **Korean War (1950–53)** — Canada fought under UN flag
- **Lester B. Pearson** — PM 1963–68; won **Nobel Peace Prize (1957)** for inventing UN peacekeeping during Suez Crisis; introduced the **Canadian flag (1965)** and **universal health care**
- **1949** — Newfoundland and Labrador joined Confederation (last province)
- **1965** — New Canadian flag adopted (red maple leaf)
- **1969** — Official Languages Act (English and French officially bilingual)
- **1982** — Constitution Act: Charter of Rights and Freedoms; patriation from Britain (Pierre Trudeau)
- **1999** — Nunavut created as Canada's third territory (Inuit homeland)
- **O Canada** became official national anthem: **1980**
- **Terry Fox** — Cancer awareness hero; started Marathon of Hope in 1980 (lost his leg to cancer, ran across Canada before cancer spread to lungs)
- **Rick Hansen** — Man in Motion World Tour (wheelchair around the world for spinal cord research)

### QnA — Module 5
**Q: What was the significance of the Battle of Vimy Ridge?**
A: All four divisions of the Canadian Corps fought together for the first time and captured the ridge — a defining moment in Canadian national identity (April 9, 1917)

**Q: When did women get the right to vote federally in Canada?**
A: 1918

**Q: What was the Winnipeg General Strike?**
A: A massive 1919 workers' strike demanding better wages and conditions — one of Canada's largest labour actions

**Q: What happened on D-Day (June 6, 1944)?**
A: Allied forces (including Canadians at Juno Beach) stormed the beaches of Normandy, France to liberate Europe from Nazi occupation

**Q: Which was the last province to join Canada?**
A: Newfoundland and Labrador (1949)

**Q: Who designed the UN peacekeeping concept and won the Nobel Peace Prize?**
A: Lester B. Pearson (1957)

**Q: When was the current Canadian flag adopted?**
A: 1965 (under PM Lester B. Pearson)

**Q: When did O Canada become the official anthem?**
A: 1980

**Q: When was the Charter of Rights and Freedoms enacted?**
A: 1982 (Constitution Act, 1982)

**Q: When was Nunavut created?**
A: 1999

**Q: Why is Terry Fox a Canadian hero?**
A: He ran the Marathon of Hope across Canada on a prosthetic leg to raise cancer awareness, despite having lost his leg to cancer

## [ ] MODULE 6 — How Government Works
> Canada is a constitutional monarchy AND a parliamentary democracy. The test loves asking about who does what. Know the three levels and three branches.

### Key Facts — The System
- Canada is a **constitutional monarchy** and a **parliamentary democracy** and a **federal state**
- **Head of State:** The Sovereign — currently **King Charles III**
- The King is represented in Canada by the **Governor General** (federally) and **Lieutenant Governor** (provincially)
- **Head of Government:** The **Prime Minister** (leader of the party with the most seats)
- **Parliament = The Crown + Senate + House of Commons**

### Three Branches of Government
| Branch | Who | Role |
|--------|-----|------|
| Executive | PM + Cabinet | Proposes laws, runs government |
| Legislative | Parliament (Senate + House of Commons) | Passes laws |
| Judicial | Courts (Supreme Court at top) | Interprets laws |

### The Senate
- **105 senators** — appointed by the Governor General on advice of the PM
- Serve until age **75**
- Called the "**sober second thought**" — reviews laws passed by House of Commons
- Represents regions, not population

### The House of Commons
- **338 elected Members of Parliament (MPs)** from 338 ridings
- Elected in federal elections
- Where laws are really made; government must have majority support here

### Three Levels of Government
| Level | Responsibilities |
|-------|-----------------|
| Federal | Defence, criminal law, banking, immigration, Indigenous affairs, postal service, fisheries, trade between provinces |
| Provincial/Territorial | Education, health care, highways, natural resources, property rights, municipal institutions |
| Municipal | Local roads, parks, local police, water, garbage |

### Supreme Court
- **9 judges** appointed by Governor General on advice of the PM
- Final court of appeal in Canada
- Includes the Chief Justice of Canada

### QnA — Module 6
**Q: Who is Canada's head of state?**
A: The Sovereign (King Charles III)

**Q: Who represents the King in Canada nationally?**
A: The Governor General

**Q: What are the three parts of Parliament?**
A: The Crown, the Senate, and the House of Commons

**Q: How many senators are there?**
A: 105

**Q: How long do senators serve?**
A: Until age 75 (they are appointed)

**Q: How many elected MPs are in the House of Commons?**
A: 338

**Q: What is the role of the Senate?**
A: To review and provide "sober second thought" on laws passed by the House of Commons

**Q: What are two federal government responsibilities?**
A: Any two of: national defence, foreign policy, criminal law, banking, immigration, postal service, fisheries, Indigenous affairs, trade between provinces

**Q: What are two provincial government responsibilities?**
A: Any two of: education, health care, highways, natural resources, property rights, municipal institutions

**Q: How many judges are on the Supreme Court of Canada?**
A: 9

**Q: What does the term "responsible government" mean?**
A: The Cabinet must have the support of the majority of elected MPs (the House of Commons) to govern

**Q: What is a Cabinet minister?**
A: An MP selected by the Prime Minister to lead and run a federal department

## [ ] MODULE 7 — Elections & Democracy
> Short module. Know the vocabulary: riding, returning officer, Elections Canada, how voting works.

### Key Facts
- Federal elections must be held at least every **4 years** (fixed date: 3rd Monday of October in the 4th year)
- Can be called earlier by dissolving Parliament
- **338 electoral districts (ridings/constituencies)** — each elects one MP
- To vote: must be **Canadian citizen, 18+**, and on voters list
- A **returning officer** runs the election in each riding
- **Elections Canada** — independent body that administers federal elections
- **First past the post** — candidate with most votes wins the riding (even without majority)
- The party with most seats forms government; leader becomes PM
- A **majority government** = more than half the seats; **minority government** = most seats but not half
- **Opposition** = parties not in power; **Leader of the Opposition** = leader of the second-largest party
- **Voter information card** is mailed to registered voters before an election
- A **polling station** is where you go to vote

### Provincial/Municipal
- Provinces have their own elected assemblies (Legislative Assemblies or National Assembly in Quebec)
- Municipal elections elect mayors and councillors

### QnA — Module 7
**Q: How often must federal elections be held?**
A: At least every 4 years

**Q: How many federal ridings (electoral districts) are there?**
A: 338

**Q: Who can vote in a federal election?**
A: Canadian citizens who are 18 years of age or older

**Q: What is a polling station?**
A: The place where you go to vote

**Q: What should you do if you don't receive a voter information card before an election?**
A: Contact Elections Canada

**Q: What is the role of Elections Canada?**
A: It is the independent body that administers federal elections

**Q: What are the parties not in power called?**
A: Opposition parties

**Q: What is a minority government?**
A: When the governing party has the most seats but fewer than half of all seats in the House of Commons

## [ ] MODULE 8 — Justice System
> The test doesn't go very deep here — just the principles and structure. Know the "innocent until proven guilty" principle cold.

### Key Facts
- **Rule of Law** — everyone, including the government, must follow the law
- **Innocent until proven guilty** — fundamental principle
- **Right to a fair trial** — legal right under the Charter
- **Right to a lawyer** — if arrested, you must be told of this right
- **Two types of offences:**
  - **Summary** — minor offences (tried quickly without jury)
  - **Indictable** — serious offences (may involve jury trial)
- **Jury duty** is a civic responsibility — citizens 18+ may be called
- **Crime = offence against the community**, not just the victim
- **Legal Aid** — available in most communities if you cannot afford a lawyer
- **Police** enforce the law; **courts** interpret and apply the law; **Parliament** makes the law
- **RCMP** (Royal Canadian Mounted Police) — federal police; also serves as provincial police in some provinces and municipal police in some cities

### QnA — Module 8
**Q: What is the principle of "innocent until proven guilty"?**
A: A person accused of a crime is considered innocent and must be proven guilty in court

**Q: What does "rule of law" mean?**
A: Everyone, including the government, must obey the law

**Q: If you cannot pay for a lawyer, how can you get legal help?**
A: Go to Legal Aid Services available in most communities

**Q: What are the two main types of criminal offences?**
A: Summary (minor) and indictable (serious) offences

**Q: What does a jury do?**
A: Hears evidence and decides whether the accused is guilty or not guilty

**Q: What is a crime in Canada considered?**
A: An offence against the whole community, not just the individual victim

## [ ] MODULE 10 — Economy & Regions
> Geography and economy together. Know what each region is known for. The test often asks "which province produces X."

### Key Facts — Economy
- Canada has a **market economy (free enterprise)**
- One of the world's **largest trading nations**
- Largest trading partner: **United States**
- Trade agreements: **CUSMA** (Canada-United States-Mexico Agreement; replaced NAFTA in 2020)
- Member of: **G7, G20, NATO, Commonwealth, La Francophonie, UN, NORAD**
- Main industries: natural resources, manufacturing, services

### Key Facts — Regions

**Atlantic Canada** (New Brunswick, Nova Scotia, PEI, Newfoundland & Labrador)
- Fisheries (cod, lobster)
- Tourism: Cabot Trail (Cape Breton), Peggy's Cove, Tidal Bore (Bay of Fundy — world's highest tides), Reversing Falls
- PEI: smallest province by area; potatoes; Anne of Green Gables
- Newfoundland: last province to join Canada (1949); oldest colony of British Empire

**Central Canada** (Ontario & Quebec)
- Home to **75% of Canada's manufacturing**
- **St. Lawrence Seaway** — connects Great Lakes to Atlantic Ocean
- Ontario: most populous province; Toronto (largest city); Ottawa (national capital)
- Quebec: largest province by area; majority French-speaking; largest city Montreal; Quebec City (oldest city)

**Prairie Provinces** (Manitoba, Saskatchewan, Alberta)
- Canada's breadbasket — wheat, canola, grains
- **Alberta:** oil sands (largest energy reserves after Saudi Arabia), cattle
- **Saskatchewan:** wheat capital; "the bread basket of the world"
- **Manitoba:** Winnipeg (gateway to the West); Red River Settlement
- Most Métis people live here

**British Columbia**
- Pacific coast; mountains; Vancouver (2nd largest city)
- Forestry (largest producer of softwood lumber)
- Mining, fishing, film industry
- **Jade** is the provincial gemstone
- Gateway to Asia-Pacific trade

**The North** (Yukon, Northwest Territories, Nunavut)
- Nunavut: largest territory; created 1999; Inuit homeland
- **Midnight sun** (summer) and **aurora borealis / Northern Lights**
- Rich in minerals and resources
- Very small population

### Key Canadian Geography Facts
- **10 provinces + 3 territories**
- **Largest province (area):** Quebec
- **Smallest province (area):** Prince Edward Island
- **Most populous province:** Ontario
- **Largest territory:** Nunavut
- **National capital:** Ottawa (Ontario)
- **Longest river:** Mackenzie River
- **Great Lakes:** shared with USA; provide fresh water and waterways — critical for trade
- **Main pulp and paper producer:** Quebec

### QnA — Module 10

A: The United States

**Q: What trade agreement does Canada have with the USA and Mexico?**
A: CUSMA (Canada-United States-Mexico Agreement)

**Q: Which region is known as Canada's "bread basket"?**
A: The Prairie provinces (especially Saskatchewan)

**Q: Which province is Canada's main producer of pulp and paper?**
A: Quebec

**Q: Which province has the largest oil reserves?**
A: Alberta (oil sands)

**Q: Which is the largest province by area?**
A: Quebec

**Q: Which is the smallest province by area?**
A: Prince Edward Island

**Q: Which was the last province to join Confederation?**
A: Newfoundland and Labrador (1949)

**Q: Which is the oldest colony of the British Empire in Canada?**
A: Newfoundland and Labrador

**Q: Why are the Great Lakes important to Canada?**
A: They provide fresh water and waterways for trade and transportation

**Q: What is Nunavut and when was it created?**
A: Canada's largest territory, created in 1999 as a homeland for the Inuit people

**Q: Where do the majority of Canadians live?**
A: In cities, primarily in the Quebec City–Windsor corridor (Central Canada)

A: British Columbia
